01 • WHERE THE DATA COMES FROM

THE ORGANIZATION

The National UFO Reporting Center (NUFORC) is an independent, long-running non-profit founded in 1974 by Robert J. Gribble. It collects voluntary public reports of unidentified aerial phenomena from around the world.

• One of the oldest continuous UFO sighting archives
• Emphasizes that most reports have conventional explanations
• Has historically shared data with researchers and agencies

THE DIGITAL SOURCE

The structured data powering this project comes from the public Hugging Face dataset:

kcimc/NUFORC

Community-curated version of the official NUFORC reports (originally scraped/transcribed from nuforc.org). Contains hundreds of thousands of rows with fields for date, location, shape, duration, narrative text, and boolean flags.

WARNING: Raw data contains heavy duplication (~591k rows → ~148k unique after dedup). Many entries are short, incomplete, or self-reported with approximate dates/locations. This is classic anecdotal narrative data — exactly the kind found in customer reviews, support tickets, and intelligence reports.

04 • VECTOR DATABASES & SEMANTIC SEARCH

What Is an Embedding?

A neural net (e5-large-v2) maps text of any length into a fixed 1024-number vector. The training goal is simple: texts with similar *meaning* end up close in vector space (measured by cosine similarity).

"Small gray being with large black eyes on a table"
and
"little grey humanoid performing medical procedure, missing time 2 hours"
→ very close vectors

"I saw a bright light that was probably Venus" → far away

Why Prefixes + Normalization Matter

  • • Documents get passage: prefix
  • • Queries get query: prefix
  • • This matches how the model was trained for asymmetric retrieval
  • • Normalization makes cosine = simple dot product (what Pinecone optimizes)

SEMANTIC vs KEYWORD

KEYWORD SEARCH
"gray alien table exam"
→ misses "small grey humanoid probed on surface"
SEMANTIC SEARCH (vector)
Same query finds the meaning, not the words.

05 • HOW THIS HELPS US UNDERSTAND LARGE NARRATIVE DATASETS

Human narratives (UFO reports, customer reviews, support tickets, medical notes, social posts, intelligence reports) share the same problems:

  • Extremely variable language
  • Implicit meaning and cultural references
  • Noisy (typos, incomplete, contradictory)
  • Hard to scale manual reading
  • Keyword search misses paraphrases
  • LLM summarization often hallucinates or cherry-picks
THE ALIEN-DB APPROACH
  1. Chunk → preserves locality + provenance (we can always point to the original report)
  2. Embed with retrieval-specialized model (e5) + proper prefixes → captures meaning
  3. Store with rich metadata (shape, abduction flag, location, etc.) → enables hybrid search
  4. Retrieve with vector similarity + metadata filters → gets the most relevant evidence
  5. Synthesize with strict grounding prompt + fabrication eval → extracts motifs while staying honest

The result is you can ask the system for patterns that "feel like" a gray medical exam and get back the actual reports that are closest in meaning, further narrowed by any flags you care about. Then a human or LLM reads the raw text and applies judgment.

This is the same technique used in business for customer stories, employee feedback, claims analysis, market research, and fraud detection — any domain where people tell stories about complex, ambiguous experiences.

THE MATH (centroid • cosine • high-sim count • k-means) — why the geometry captures meaning
Unit vectors + cosine = dot product
All 1024-d vectors are normalized (length 1). cos(A,B) = A·B. 0.82 means the two chunks' meaning-vectors point in almost the same direction (shared latent features the model learned during pretraining on massive text).
Centroid / rep vector
For benchmarks: we embed our 3 "perfect example" stories once. For a k-means cluster: the centroid is the average of the member vectors — the "center of gravity" of that micro-cloud in meaning space.
High-sim count (the "20775" etc)
How many of the 21,179 real chunks have cos > ~0.78 to the rep vector? This is prevalence of that semantic profile, not a tag count. It is the quantitative "how common is this motif".
k-means on 21k×1024 for rare
Partitions into 100 groups. We discard the large obvious clusters and keep only size 2–12. These are tight but uncommon neighborhoods (e.g. cube + silent + instant accel, or vampire-like + energy drain). The "why" callout describes the shared descriptors that co-occurred in the embedding.
Graded not binary. A chunk can be 0.81 to gray-exam AND 0.44 to triangle. Soft participation in multiple motifs. The quotes you see on click/roll are the exact chunk_text strings that were turned into those vectors.
